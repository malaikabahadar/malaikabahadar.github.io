Statistics Research Internship

During my summer research internship at the University of Leeds, I immersed myself in the quantitative analysis of survey data collected from over a thousand physics graduates. Collaborating closely with professors and a PhD researcher, I engineered and refined statistical models in SPSS and Excel, looking at a dataset of over 100,000 data points.

I performed statistical analyses in SPSS, including data transformation and hypothesis testing, engineering new variables—such as GRADE_TEXT, DEGREE_TEXT, and MINORITY_TEXT—to numerically encode qualitative responses, enabling the use of different statistical techniques. My analysis revealed weak correlations overall.

I also supported the design and testing of interventions aimed at improving female participation in physics, including the running of differnt workshops. This work culminated in a research essay showcased at the prestigious National STEM Learning Centre, earning an award and formal recognition, reflecting both the quality of my analysis and its contribution to understanding the trajectories of physics graduates.

Analysis of Strategic Decision-Making in Rock-Paper-Scissors

For my independent project on decision science, I designed and executed a medium-scale experiment with 250 participants, focusing on probabilistic patterns and strategic adaptation in repeated Rock-Paper-Scissors games. This project bridged mathematics, psychology and game theory: I modelled player behaviour using Markov processes and examined deviations from pure randomness using statistical tests and behaviour clustering techniques.

The data revealed insights into human strategies, such as conditional response tendencies and sequence prediction. To operationalise these findings, I developed a Python bot for Discord that dynamically adjusted its strategy based on real-time opponent data, ultimately achieving a 60% win rate against human players—a significant improvement over chance and a demonstration of how mathematical modelling can be harnessed to outperform in competitive, iterative environments.

How Far Can Mathematical Methods Predict Human Behaviour in Simple Mathematical Games?

The Mayor of Bradford personally approached me to commend my work, describing it as one of the best they had ever encountered. This dissertation examined the true probabilities, and hence the accuracy, of the mathematical models behind the games of Rock, Paper, Scissors, Split or Steal, Coin Toss and Pick a Number 1-10.

I examined the games against a range of factors that could affect their likelihood of efficiently and reliably predicting human behaviour.
