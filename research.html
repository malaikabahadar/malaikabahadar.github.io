Analysis of Strategic Decision-Making in Rock-Paper-Scissors

I designed and executed an experiment with 250 participants, each playing five rounds against me. Every participant received a unique sequence generated by a random number generator to keep the data unbiased and varied.

To model how people adapt and make decisions, I built a hybrid system that combined first order and second order Markov chains with Bayesian updating. This allowed the algorithm to not only recognise short-term reactions but also adapt over time to a player’s evolving strategy. The experiment ran in three phases: on the first move, the model played randomly with a 50 percent chance of throwing rock, 30 percent for paper, and 20 percent for scissors. On the second move, it used a pooled first order Markov model trained on 1000 transitions. From the third move onwards, it switched to a pooled second order Markov model with 750 transitions, refined through weighted Bayesian updating so the system could learn from each opponent’s style in real time.

Since some of the counts in my count matrices for the second order model had zero entries, I used Laplace smoothing to stabilise the probabilities and prevent errors. Once the model was complete, I tested it against unseen player data and a random generator to check its accuracy. I then compared the hybrid model’s win rate with several baselines, true random play, first order Markov only, and second order Markov only, using the Wilcoxon signed rank test at a five percent confidence interval, as my testing group of twenty participants was below the usual threshold for a t-test.

The hybrid model achieved a consistent sixty percent win rate, significantly outperforming all baselines. What fascinated me most was what this revealed: human randomness isn’t really random, and this finding went out on to inspire many further projects.

Statistics Research Internship

During my summer research internship at the University of Leeds, I immersed myself in the quantitative analysis of survey data collected from over a thousand physics graduates. Collaborating closely with professors and a PhD researcher, I engineered and refined statistical models in SPSS and Excel, looking at a dataset of over 100,000 data points.

I performed statistical analyses in SPSS, including data transformation and hypothesis testing, engineering new variables—such as GRADE_TEXT, DEGREE_TEXT, and MINORITY_TEXT—to numerically encode qualitative responses, enabling the use of different statistical techniques. My analysis revealed weak correlations overall.

I also supported the design and testing of interventions aimed at improving female participation in physics, including the running of differnt workshops. This work culminated in a research essay showcased at the prestigious National STEM Learning Centre, earning an award and formal recognition, reflecting both the quality of my analysis and its contribution to understanding the trajectories of physics graduates.

How Far Can Mathematical Methods Predict Human Behaviour in Simple Mathematical Games?

The Mayor of Bradford personally approached me to commend my work, describing it as one of the best they had ever encountered. This dissertation examined the true probabilities, and hence the accuracy, of the mathematical models behind the games of Rock, Paper, Scissors, Split or Steal, Coin Toss and Pick a Number 1-10.

I examined the games against a range of factors that could affect their likelihood of efficiently and reliably predicting human behaviour.
